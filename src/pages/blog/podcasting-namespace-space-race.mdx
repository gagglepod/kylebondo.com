# Paragraphs

Apollo 11 astronauts Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin became the first men to walk on the Moon on July 20, 1969. Three years later, on December 11, 1972, Apollo 17 astronauts Gene Cernan and Harrison Schmitt were the last men to walk on the Moon.

That was 50 years ago.

Why did we not go back?

Some say it was a lack of innovation. The technologies that took us to the Moon were incredibly expensive, difficult to manufacture, and required too much effort to optimize.

In other words, it would have been hard to go back without significant support and effort. When we hadn't reached the Moon, that support was there in spades. However, once "Moon fever" had passed that support faded away. Instead of returning to the Moon, we focused on optimizing what we did have which was an interest in ending the war in Vietnam and healing the country.

I believe we didn't go back to the Moon because we, as a country, decided to focus on other things.

We put the space race on autopilot.

As a footnote to our space race to the Moon, as of 2022, were are finally going back. The Artemis I launch in November 2022, and marked the first major spaceflight focused on returning exploration to the Moon since Apollo. Soon, astronauts will go back to the Moon too, all thanks to "recent" innovation in space technology.

### That's the key term: innovation

It's a word that everyone uses but few people know what it means. I like the way Simon Sinek describes innovation:

"Innovation is not efficient. Innovation is trial and error. Innovation is experimentation. Innovation is testing. Innovation is doing something that fails and fails and fails... and sticking with it because we can see the light at the end of the tunnel and we are not going to walk away from this even though if failed the first two or three times."

Innovation makes people uncomfortable because of the failure part. Innovation means, "this could fail", and often does fail. I think this is why most people focus on optimization over innovation. Optimization is safe. You take something someone else has created and make it... well... better! You shave off a corner here, or make an adjustment there, and waa-laa! Something new! But it's not new, it's only a better version of what already was.

Innovation, on the other hand, is hard. It is a struggle between experimentation and vision that often results in failure. We don't like failure. In fact, we hate failure so much that we find ways to avoid it. This is why optimization is so appealing. We don't have to create, only correct. Creation, the source of all innovation, requires us to learn through failing over and over again. When you're talking about rockets, those lessons get expensive very quickly. Especially when an astronaut's life or on the line.

I think the act of innovation can be summed up by a quote from the Roman emperor Marcus Aurelius, who said, "The obstacle is the way." While optimization has its place, it is innovation, the process of creation, that we should be after. Unfortunately, as Thomas Edison is credited with saying, "Opportunity is missed by most people because it is dressed in overalls and looks like work." This is the dark side of innovation in that it takes work -- hard work -- to fail enough times before finding success.

This makes failure, the art of "this may not work", a key part of the path through the obstacle that must be overcome. This kind of failure is what Thomas Edison once mused was not even true failure. He took an optimistic approach by saying, "I have not failed 10,000 times -- I've successfully found 10,000 ways that will not work."

### This is the current state of Podcasting

Podcasting technology has become stuck in a rut. For the past decade, we have been optimizing podcasting in every way possible, not innovating.

We allowed podcasting to be placed on autopilot.

However, what if I were to tell you that podcasting is on the verge of a renaissance? That the nature of podcasting -- at least the way you know it today -- is about to radically change? What if I told you that somewhere in the dark corners of the Internet there HAVE been people working on podcasting innovation?

Nonsense?

Heresy?

_Witch Craft?_

We'll see.

First, I want to talk about how podcasting is done today.

You think up some thoughts, record your voice talk about those thoughts, edit the audio file of those thoughts, save that audio file to a media host, and share the location of that audio file with the Internet. Then you hope someone out in the universe takes the time to find, download, and listen to those thoughts. Then maybe, just maybe, they will take the time to give you feedback about those thoughts, either by email or by purchasing your course (place a rimshot here).

Then there is the listener side of this equation. They have to find your show, find an episode, press play, listen long enough to decide if they like it or now, and then subscribe or follow your podcast so they can be notified when you've published something new.

Most podcasters understand this at a high level.

The only variation to this blueprint is the content itself. You add guests here, put music there, or find a way to include notes that might get read. Fundamentally, however, it has not changed in a long, long time. This is the current realm of the podcasting industrial complex. The top influencers in podcasting settled for optimization by focusing on how to make the pieces within the process better.
Here are some examples:

- Think of directories. Podcasting search is defined by those companies that control the most eyeballs. Since they can decide winners and losers by controlling who sees what, Search is as good as it will ever get. Optimized.
- Think of audio hosting. There are hosting companies out there that will help you share your audio files, give you all sorts of stats, post to all the directories that matter, and even help you stuff your show with dynamic ads. Optimized.
- Now think of audio software. We have software-driven channel controls, compression algorithms, open-source options, and YouTube experts to teach you everything you wanted to know. Heck, you can even record your audio using only your phone and have a computer remove the ums and ahs for you the output a transcript. Optimized.

All good things.

But what is missing from this exhaustive list of technology improvements?

### Listener Experience

Every advancement in podcasting technology over the past 10 years has been for the podcaster, not the listener. Sure, you could argue that by improving the podcaster's ability to produce audio content you help the listener by providing them with an endless stream of content. But that's not good enough.

No, the podcaster has everything they need to make content now. Now it is the listener's turn. It's time to consider improvements that focus on the listener's experience.

The trick now is figuration out how we innovate for the listener experience. Not optimize the listener experience, but innovate it.

### You start with understanding disruption

When everyone gets used to a certain way of doing something, and that something doesn't change for a long, long time, we say that it is ripe for disruption. It is an opportunity to change something for the better by exploiting the weakness in the current system and improving (or simplifying) the entire process.

- We did taxis for almost two centuries. Then came Uber. Goodbye smelly taxis and hello strangers in their own cars who actually show up.
- We did rentals for generations. Then came Airbnb. Goodbye, questionable hotels or your friend's couch, and hello your own furnished house for a week.
- We even did the low-orbit Space Shuttle boondoggle for most of my childhood. Then came SpaceX and reusable rocket boosters. Goodbye billion-dollar one-shots and hello brand new space transport industry.

Right now, podcasting -- at least the way we have been doing podcasting for the past 10 years -- is on that same chopping block.

Podcasting has become ripe for disruption because we've been doing the same thing the same way for years. This has created weaknesses in the podcasting industrial complex. The structure has become brittle because everyone in the industry is waiting for THEM to make the next move.

### They are never going to make the next move

That means it's time for the rest of us to make the next move.

It's time for us to disrupt podcasting by adopting Podcasting 2.0.

Podcasting 2.0 -- an industry disruption concept heralded by Adam Curry and Dave Jones via their podcast Podcasting 2.0, along with a dozen or so developers, engineers, and evangelists -- is summed up by a quote from Tom Rossi as a _"[100-percent focus] on improving the podcasting experience in an open-standard way that allows every player to innovate faster and better than any one company could do on their own. This is our best bet at avoiding one company emerging as the monopoly of podcasting."_

Player innovation -- that's a big deal, mainly because it's the pointy end of the podcasting spear that directly impacts the listener. There is no point in producing a podcast without a way for your listener to _"find"_ and _"play"_ that podcast.

Why is Podcasting 2.0 so important? Because it is more than a technological change. It is a philosophical change to how we use and understand podcasting. It is imagining a world where big tech no longer sets the rules for podcasting and a real move back to open-source podcasting.

The adoption of the Podcasting 2.0 philosophy has already started. Podcasters, podcast media hosts, and podcast app developers have been building, failing, building, failing, and building again for years. However, within the past two years, they are beginning to see the innovation they have been working for. After finding 10,000 ways to not innovate podcasting, they found the one way to innovate podcasting: The Podcasting 2.0 Namespace.

For the longest time, Apple's iTunes Namespace has been driving the podcasting train. Well, Apple and their 500-pound Gorilla called the iPhone. Within the Apple iPhone closed ecosystem, Apple has controlled the main way podcast media is read into podcast players by greatly influencing what data will and will not be used by Apple podcast players.

This is called gatekeeping and Apple can gatekeep with the best of them. Their market power gives them the capability (and the leverage) to push podcasters, podcast media companies, and podcast player developers in a direction that benefits only Apple. However, for far too many years, Apple has done something far worse than use its influence to control podcasting: they have done nothing.

Nada.

Zilch.

They forced the podcasting industrial complex to adopt a format that benefited Apple apps and then quietly put turned their podcasting attention on autopilot.

Sound familiar?

Since then, that format -- that iTunes Namespace -- has atrophied. We all waited. Waited for the sleeping 500-pound gorilla to wake up and realize that changes were needed. But nothing happened. We asked, we demanded, we even begged. For over a decade we accepted the tyranny of big tech and got nothing. And the reason we got nothing was simple: Apple doesn't have to do anything. They've optimized the market to their benefit.

Steve Jobs even told the world that podcasting was, _"... the concatenation of the word iPod and Broadcasting"_ at the All Things D3 conference in 2005. He, along with Apple, thought they owned this technology too. However, the adoption of Podcasting 2.0 namespace will mean the end of that control. Because it will represent more than just Apple. It will represent the entire podcasting ecosystem that has grown despite Apple's reluctance to innovate.

I believe that when the podcasting namespace blows wide open in 2023, three very amazing things happen:

1. It will mark the end of gatekeepers. It will take central control away from big tech and remove the constraints over what a podcast feed can deliver to the listener via a Podcasting 2.0-enabled podcast player. In other words, it will allow new relationships to form between the podcaster creator and their listeners without a tyrannical middle-man dictating how that connection will be made.
2. It will allow podcasting application developers to experiment with the overall listener experience by leveraging the influx of new podcasting 2.0 information. The current Podcasting 2.0 Namespace already includes tags that allow for new listening behaviors. These behaviors include "Value for Value" connections where listeners can provide direct messages and monetary feedback to podcasters.
3. Podcasting will start growing again. Experiments in just what the Podcasting 2.0 namespace can do will open up podcasting to new listener experiences that we have yet to think up. The namespace has already started expanding into monetization/funding via cryptocurrency, geo-location for local podcast directories, alternate version delivery, live podcasting notifications, transcript and chapter augmentation, and even expansive biographies of hosts and guests. Or, to sum it up in a word: innovation!

One simple change, the adoption of Podcasting 2.0 namespace, will be the cornerstone on which the next podcasting revolution will be built.

### This is the namespace space race

On one side we have the Podcasting 2.0 namespace, out in the open for everyone to use, versus the closed ecosystem of the big tech companies that make up the podcasting industrial complex.

I strongly believe the future of podcasting depends on an open, community-driven development of the Podcasting 2.0 namespace. As more podcasters learn about this movement, it will create more and more healthy debates on what the future of podcasting should look like.

So I leave you with this final thought: who controls podcasting?

I see podcasting as the last gasp of true intellectual freedom, over the next year, those who love podcasting will ultimately have to decide where podcasting innovation lives.

I once believed that big tech would be the ones to innovate podcasting. Unfortunately, I have come to realize that these companies have become the same bloated big companies they once claimed to be revolting against. They are now the new gatekeepers who focus only on optimizing the status quo or canceling those that displease them.

No, the real innovation in podcasting will not come from big tech. I see now that true podcasting innovation will have to come from the podcasting community itself. It will be the rag-tag bunch of podcasting enthusiasts who have been tinkering in their Internet garages, experimenting, testing, and learning through trial and error, what the future of podcasting can be.

True podcasting innovation will be driven by those that stuck with it until they had something that was worth showing to the world.

This is my prediction for podcasting: An explosion in the adoption of the Podcasting 2.0 Namespace.
Let the Namespace Space Race begin in the name of Listener Experience!
